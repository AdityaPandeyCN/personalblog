My Google Summer of Code 2025 Selection with CERN

Posted on Sat 28 June 2025 in Blog

Welcome to my GSoC journey! I'm thrilled to share the story of how my fascination with the Higgs boson discovery led me to an incredible opportunity with CERN.

What is this blog about?

This blog will document my Google Summer of Code experience with CERN HSF and Princeton University's Compiler Research Team, where I'll be working on "Using ROOT in the field of genome sequencing."

From Higgs Boson Dreams to CERN Reality

Ever since I learned about the Higgs boson discovery in 2012, I've been fascinated by CERN and the incredible technology behind particle physics research. The idea that scientists could smash particles together at nearly the speed of light and discover fundamental truths about our universe felt like magic. That moment sparked a dream - I wanted to work at CERN someday.

Fast forward to 2025, and I'm excited to share that I've been selected for Google Summer of Code with CERN HSF and Princeton University's Compiler Research Team! What makes this even more special is that I was actually selected for two different projects and had to make a choice.

When GSoC 2025 opened applications, CERN HSF was at the top of my list. I spent weeks exploring their project ideas, looking for something that would align with my interests while contributing meaningfully to the physics community. Two projects caught my attention:

Project 1: TMVA SOFIE - HLS4ML Integration This project focused on machine learning inference optimization within ROOT's TMVA toolkit. I had to solve a competency test for this project, demonstrating my understanding of C++ and Python programming, machine learning frameworks, and ROOT's TMVA system.

Project 2: Using ROOT in Genome Sequencing The second project was about applying ROOT's data processing capabilities - the same technology used to analyze LHC data - to genomic data storage and analysis. This interdisciplinary approach immediately caught my attention.

After weeks of preparation, proposal writing, and technical discussions with mentors, the results came in. On May 1st, I received an email from Valentin Volkl that began: "Dear Aditya, congratulations on being selected for GSoC in our org - twice even!"

I couldn't believe it. Being selected for one GSoC project is an achievement, but being selected for two felt surreal. I chose "Using ROOT in the field of genome sequencing" because it bridges computational physics and bioinformatics, applies ROOT's technology to a novel domain, and addresses real-world challenges in genomic data processing.

This isn't my first GSoC participation. My previous experience taught me valuable lessons about project scope management, community engagement, documentation, and open source contribution. This experience gave me confidence in tackling another challenging project.

My Project Overview

My selected project focuses on extending GeneROOT capabilities - applying CERN's ROOT framework to genomic data analysis. The core objectives include:

  • Compression Analysis: Comparing ROOT's compression with standard genomics tools like Samtools
  • RNTuple Migration: Exploring ROOT's next-generation RNTuple format for genomic data storage
  • Performance Benchmarking: Analyzing file splitting techniques and storage optimization
  • Cross-domain Innovation: Bringing particle physics data analysis expertise to bioinformatics

Why This Matters: ROOT routinely achieves 10-50% file size reductions and multiple times faster read throughput. Applied to the massive genomic datasets generated by modern sequencing technologies, this could revolutionize how biological research is conducted.

Working with CERN Mentors

I'm incredibly fortunate to work with world-class mentors:

  • Martin Vasilev from University of Plovdiv
  • Jonas Rembser from CERN
  • Fons Rademakers from CERN

These are the same brilliant minds who've been instrumental in developing ROOT and analyzing the data that led to groundbreaking discoveries in particle physics.

What's next?

As I prepare to start this project, I'm filled with excitement and gratitude. That young student who was amazed by the Higgs boson discovery never imagined they'd someday be working on CERN technology, contributing to open-source software that could impact both particle physics and biology.

In the coming posts, I plan to share:

  • Technical deep-dives into ROOT's RNTuple format
  • Performance benchmarks comparing ROOT with traditional genomics tools
  • Challenges and breakthroughs in applying physics computing to biology
  • Lessons learned from working with the CERN development community

I'll also share updates on the technical challenges I face while progressing through GSoC and the lessons I learn along the way.

Thank you for joining me on this adventure! I'm looking forward to documenting this incredible journey at the intersection of particle physics and genomics.
